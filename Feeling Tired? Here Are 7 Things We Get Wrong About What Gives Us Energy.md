# References

> [!QUOTE] Original Capture
> Misconception #1: Exercise is exhausting
> Acute bouts of exercise generally have an energizing effect
> Exercise boosts cognition, lifts mood and has strong effects on depression, anxiety, ADHD and more
> The long-term effects of consistent exercise are even more positive
> 
> Misconception #2: Introverts are drained by socializing
> Researchers tested this idea by measuring the extroversion of 146 participants and then asking them to anticipate how participating in twenty minutes of socializing with several strangers would impact their energy
> Nearly everyone felt better after socializing, except for the extreme introverts (who felt neither better, nor worse)
> 
> Misconception #3: Scrolling on your phone is a great way to relax
> the largest problem seems to be that screens are so compelling that they shorten our sleep
> while they can be relaxing, they often fail to provide the experiences of mastery and meaning that we can get from other leisure activities
> 
> Misconception #4: Long hours at work inevitably lead to burnout
> When workers have autonomy and meaningful work, high demands no longer result in fatigue
> Fatigue may be better seen as an emotion that arises when you spend too much of your limited attention on activities that don’t feel intrinsically worthwhile to you
> 
> Misconception #5: Alcohol helps you sleep better
> While alcohol (and other chemical sedatives) do make it easier to become unconscious, they disrupt the delicate neural choreography that gives sleep its value in restoring our energy
> 
> Misconception #6: Venting helps you relieve stress
> Catharsis, or the release of anger as a way to prevent the emotion from burning you up, was long seen as a necessary release from this internal tension
> Emotions and thoughts form a self-reinforcing loop. 
> 	Thus, angry behaviors reinforce angry thoughts. 
> 	The key is to break this loop, not reinforce it
> 
> (Partial) Misconception #7: Coffee boosts your energy (in the long-run)
> Caffeine boosts alertness, reduces sleepiness, increases endurance and muscular performance, and has many other cognitive benefits
> Results of a study suggest that much of caffeine’s boosting effects can be seen not as a stable long-term improvement, but simply as temporarily undoing the withdrawal state
