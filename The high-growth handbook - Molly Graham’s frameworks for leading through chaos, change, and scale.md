> [!QUOTE] Original Capture
> “Give away your Legos.” This is Molly’s core framework for scaling yourself as a leader at a high-growth company. The idea is that the things you built and love doing—your “Legos”—must be handed off to others as the company grows, even when it feels painful. Molly developed this after watching leaders at Google and Facebook cling to work they enjoyed, becoming bottlenecks that slowed their teams and stunted their own growth.
> 
> The best careers look like J-curves, not stairs. The most valuable career moves feel like jumping off cliffs. You’ll fall for six to nine months, feeling incompetent and asking “dumb” questions. But when you climb out, you’ll reach heights the “stairs” approach could never access. Molly’s advice: “Different kinds of fear tell you different things. Financial fear might be worth listening to, but the fear of ‘I can’t do this’ is a flashing green light to go for it.”
> 
> Culture is 80% defined by the founder’s personality. After years of trying to shape culture at Facebook, Molly realized: “Facebook is Mark. Google is Larry and Sergey.” Your job as an operator isn’t to create culture but to articulate and extend the culture the founder naturally creates. Writing values that don’t match the founder’s actual behavior only creates cultural dissonance. Culture isn’t what you say; it’s what you do.
> 
> Escalation is a tool, not a failure. When two people with equal power disagree, they often waste weeks debating. Instead, go together to someone with more context or authority as soon as you’re stuck. This isn’t tattling—it’s using management for its intended purpose: unblocking teams. As Zuckerberg taught Molly, “escalation saves time and creates clarity.”
> 
> Snorkel before you scuba. 80% of team issues stem from structural company problems (unclear goals, roles, expectations) or team dynamics, not individual performance. So before assuming the person is flawed or unfit, start at the surface level by asking, “Does everyone know what their job is and what success looks like?” Only dive deeper into interpersonal or individual issues if structural fixes aren’t the issue.
> 
> No company needs more than three goals. Facebook ran on just three goals for five years while growing from 500 to 5,500 employees: growth (MAUs), engagement, and revenue. One goal must “win in a fight” to create true clarity (at Facebook, engagement trumped all). Goals should be simple enough that “an intern who started Monday should understand them,” and they should hurt—if your prioritization process isn’t painful, you’re not making real tradeoffs.
> 
> Effective goal-setting requires six rules to create real alignment: goals must be specific, measurable, time-bound, public, few in number, and actually used to make decisions. Most companies fail because they set too many goals, make them too vague, or never reference them after the kickoff meeting. Molly’s framework, refined through work with Mark Zuckerberg and Bret Taylor, emphasizes that goals aren’t just aspirational—they’re the filter for every decision, meeting, and priority throughout the quarter.
> 
> In rapidly scaling organizations, you need to over-communicate by a factor of 10x. Molly observed at Facebook that Zuck would repeat the same message in multiple formats (all-hands, email, 1:1s, team meetings), because people need to hear something seven to 10 times before it actually sinks in. Leaders often assume everyone heard and understood them the first time, but in chaotic, fast-growing environments, repetition is the only way to create shared understanding.
> 
> The biggest lesson Molly learned from Sergey and Larry at Google was to stay relentlessly focused on the user, not the org chart or internal politics. They would constantly ask, “What does the user need?” and let that question drive every decision, even if it meant reorganizing teams or killing projects that people loved. This user-centric forcing function cuts through complexity and creates clarity when everything else feels ambiguous.
> 
> The biggest mistake leaders make during hypergrowth is trying to preserve how things used to work. Molly saw this repeatedly at Google, Facebook, and Quip: The leaders who clung to old structures, old ways of working, or old team cultures became obstacles. The companies that scaled successfully were the ones where leaders embraced that their job was to constantly reinvent how the org operated.
