> [!QUOTE] Original Capture
> There’s a force that pulls organizations toward mediocrity and corruption—“the force no one controls but everyone obeys.” It’s not greed or aging; it’s “financial gravity”: structural pressure from governance. Eric describes how a friend could literally taste when a restaurant had been taken over by private equity. The same pattern erodes brands, removes founders, and turns products into extractive machines.
> 
> Only 20% of venture-backed founders are still CEO three years post-IPO. Follow standard VC and legal advice, and you have an 80% chance of being pushed out. Everyone thinks they’re the exception—but statistically, you’re not. Eric mentions a founder who ignored this, went public successfully, and was fired five months later when the stock dipped and investors reversed course.
> 
> The key question isn’t what protections you need—it’s when. At every stage, founders are told “not yet”: lawyers say wait for PMF, Series A VCs say later, growth VCs say it’ll hurt fundraising, bankers at IPO say bundle it later, then the CFO at S-1 says, “Sorry, too late.” It’s never the right time—which means you have to do it early.
> 
> Most corporate charters say companies exist to pursue “any lawful activity,” which effectively means maximizing shareholder value. This shareholder primacy hasn’t always been how it works. It has only dominated for about 40 years. Today, boards are legally obligated to take the highest offer—even harmful ones. Eric points out how Philip Morris acquired Vectura, destroying it within three years.
> 
> The simplest protection: become a “public benefit corporation.” This is different from a B Corp. It’s a short legal filing where you define your actual purpose. It doesn’t guarantee good behavior, but it protects you if investors push you to act against your mission. It’s part of the governance “armor” used by companies like Anthropic, with essentially no downside.
> 
> Anthropic shows how strong governance allows you to move faster. Built into its structure from day one, it allows independent AI safety experts—without financial incentives—to influence decisions. This enables Anthropic to refuse risky model releases or lucrative deals. Across the AI industry, no other serious player is using standard governance.
> 
> Three things founders should do now: (1) become a public benefit corporation, (2) add a director’s oath tied to mission, and (3) implement mission-protective provisions (e.g. founder control, board votes). Optionally, reserve equity for a nonprofit foundation. These are easiest early, before investor constraints kick in.
> 
> “Harder is easier.” Cloudflare gave SSL away free even though it was their top conversion driver. Conversion dropped briefly, top-of-funnel grew 10x, and they became a $70B company. Principled decisions always lose on ROI spreadsheets in the short run—that’s why structural protection matters more than values statements.
