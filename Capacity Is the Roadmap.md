# References

> [!QUOTE] Original Capture
> People like to talk about priorities as if the main problem is choosing what matters. In practice, the deterministic factor is capacity. Team capacity. System capacity. The share you lose to maintenance, interruptions, [coordination](https://yusufaytas.com/cracking-coding-bottlenecks), and keeping the machine fit to run. Ignoring these physical limits turns an ambitious roadmap into a collective illusion.
> 
> Work should not enter as raw demand. It has to become clear enough to compete for real capacity.
> 
> A request is not a case - "The customer asked for this", "Sales says this is urgent"
> 	These are signals, not roadmap-worthy items yet - they require more structure
> 	It requires a problem statement, evidence that the problem is real, an expected outcome, a dependency view, and at least some sense of what it will displace
> 	Till we have the above, it is not ready to compete for capacity
> 	
> Good shaping exposes assumptions, narrows the idea, breaks it down into smaller parts, and separates must-haves from luxury
> 	Valuable? Usable? Feasible? Viable?
> 	If above questions are unanswered, it is still in the discovery period
> 	
> Most roadmap dishonesty starts when teams discuss additions without naming displacement
> 	Don't add without removing
> 
> Estimates force boundaries
> 	Smaller versions become possible
> 	Turns vague enthusiasm into something discussable
> 	
> Allocate capacity explicitly since it is the real roadmap
> 	If it remains invisible, it will get allocated implicitly - by the loudest stakeholder, nearest escalation, or latest surprise
> 	
> Use named investment buckets - customer facing work, reliability, eng health, platform, enablement work, etc
> 	Make the mix visible so the people can see the tradeoffs
> 	Buckets expose the zero-sum nature of the plan
> 	Note: Vendor solution shifts the cost to different buckets, it doesn't remove it entirely
> 
> Tech debt belongs with normal delivery, but larger eng health work needs explicit room
> 	Otherwise, it will be neglected by default
> 	Work here is diverse - maintenance, leverage for future delivery, time-bomb work, etc
> 
> Interruptions must be explicitly budgeted for using reserve capacity buckets - the category will be unpredictable
> 	Oncall, postmorterms, forced migrations, etc - some of the typically unplanned work
> 
> What gets a protected slice of capacity is what the org truly values
> 	Teams should be able to see where leadership is putting real money and where the company is willing to bet the house
> 
> Every shift has a restart cost - roadmap changes waste partial capacity that was already invested elsewhere
> 
> A real plan says what will happen, what will not, and why. Anything less is paperwork pretending to be planning
